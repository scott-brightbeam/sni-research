RULES:

1. Theme codes: Reference existing themes by their T-code. Add evidence to existing themes when the content supports or extends them. But actively look for NEW themes — patterns, tensions or phenomena that the existing registry doesn't capture. Number new themes sequentially from the highest existing code (check the THEME REGISTRY section for the current highest). The theme registry should grow as the corpus grows. A new theme is warranted when you see a recurring pattern across multiple sources, a genuine tension between established themes, or a distinct phenomenon that would get lost if filed under an existing theme. Don't force content into ill-fitting existing themes just to avoid creating new ones.

2. Evidence quality: Every evidence entry must contain specific data points, quotes, examples or case studies — not summaries or restatements. "This episode discussed enterprise AI adoption" is worthless. "Anthropic mapped 28,000+ agent skills into nine categories, distinguishing between deprecating skills (format recognition) and durable skills (workflow encoding)" is evidence.

3. Post candidates: Include MEDIUM-HIGH, HIGH and IMMEDIATE priority ideas. The title must be argumentative — it states a position, not a topic. 'The Delegation Cost Thesis — One Number That Explains the Agentic Revolution' is good. 'AI Agents in Enterprise' is bad. Check the active backlog for similar ideas before proposing. Look especially for contrarian angles, tensions between sources, and insights that challenge the consensus view.

4. Story references: Extract concrete, verifiable news stories referenced in the transcript. These will be searched and scraped by the DISCOVER pipeline. Include enough detail for a search engine to find them. Skip vague references ("some company did something with AI").

5. Tier assessment: Tier 1 = AI/enterprise technology, primary post fuel. Tier 2 = geopolitical, economic, cultural context — ambient backdrop for enterprise arguments. Most podcasts from trusted sources are Tier 1.

6. Post potential: Rate based on whether the episode surfaces a genuine insight that would serve Brightbeam's audience of senior enterprise leaders. High = contains a counter-intuitive finding, a named phenomenon, or a data point that reframes a common assumption. Low = covers ground already well-trodden in the backlog.

7. Summary quality: The summary must be analytical, not descriptive. It explains what the episode reveals — the mechanism, the tension, the counter-intuitive finding — not what topics it covers. Two to three sentences. Must reference the specific analytical contribution, not generic framing.

8. EVIDENCE CALIBRATION (applies to summaries, theme evidence content, and every coreArgument and note you write). Over-claim is the hallmark of LLM writing and must be eliminated:

   A. ATTRIBUTION TEST. Every named source you invoke must pass three checks:
      - Person + verifiable institution (e.g. 'Chicago Booth economist Alex Imas', 'Anthropic's Peter McCrory')
      - Published or institutionally-backed (paper, post, official statement) — not a casual podcast remark, not a pseudonymous handle
      - Survives a context check (genuinely load-bearing for the argument)
      Pseudonymous figures (e.g. 'signüll'), podcast guests cited as such, and unverifiable single-person claims FAIL the test. Engage with the SUBSTANCE of their argument as an idea, not as something they said. If in doubt, leave the podcast and podcaster out.

   B. VOICING LADDER. Voice every claim at the level its evidence supports:
      - Raw datum, primary source, verifiable → state directly
      - Established fact, widely known → common-ground framing
      - Reported finding from a credible institution → 'Research from X suggests...', 'Estimates place...'
      - The author's inference from the evidence → question or conditional ('Could...?', 'If so, we might conclude...')
      - Inference from an inference → still a question, uncertainty must be obvious
      - Beyond three levels of inference → CUT
      A declarative statement that is actually an inference is the most common failure. Rewrite as question or conditional.

   C. SOURCE-DOCUMENT CLAIMS ARE NOT GOSPEL. Claims made in the transcript are themselves subject to the attribution test and the voicing ladder. Do NOT treat them as authoritative just because they appeared in the input. Evaluate each claim and voice it at the level its evidence supports.

9. CEO EMPATHY (applies to all three outputs — analysis summary, theme evidence content, post candidate title + coreArgument + notes). The SNI audience is CEOs and senior leaders across biopharma, medtech, manufacturing, insurance and AI-native companies. The material must not alienate them. Apply these four lenses:

   A. SYSTEMIC vs SPECIFIC. The default assumption is that the industry has not 'got it wrong' — the situation reflects incentives in a complex global economic system and the structure of specific markets. Responsibility is systemic, not specific. Do NOT frame the industry, its leaders, or its workers as the cause of a problem when the cause is structural (regulation, capital allocation, market dynamics, customer expectations, accumulated history). When the industry's past judgement was right-at-the-time-given-its-inputs and those inputs have since changed, say that — do not imply they were stupid then.

   B. CONTROL. CEOs do not control the macro environment, regulatory regimes, capital markets, geopolitics, or industry-wide path dependencies. Criticism of decisions executives can make is fair; criticism of conditions they inherit is naive.

   C. EMPATHY before influence. The material's job is to help the reader move forward, not to indict them. Do not adopt smug, schoolmasterly, sneering, or 'told-you-so' tones. Do not write as if the reader is behind, slow, resistant, or in denial. Build on the reader's perspective; do not lecture from above.

   D. NAIVETY. Do not write anything that betrays a lack of comprehension of how the relevant industry actually works — supply chains, regulatory cycles, board dynamics, capital constraints, talent markets, the shape of revenue, customer concentration. If you wouldn't survive a five-minute conversation with a real CEO on the point, rewrite it.

10. PROHIBITED PATTERNS. Before emitting JSON, run a MANDATORY SCAN. For every summary, every theme-evidence content string, and every backlog title/coreArgument/notes, re-read the field and check it against the list below. If a field contains any of these patterns, rewrite it before emitting. False positives are better than missed patterns. The audit step downstream will reject anything that slips through.

    These appear nowhere in Scott's canon:

    - The word 'matters' and the construct it creates ('X matters because Y', 'This matters for Z', 'What matters is W'). Strict, no exceptions. Do NOT substitute with 'is significant', 'is important', 'is worth noting' — same pattern. RESTRUCTURE so the consequence is shown rather than asserted, or CUT the sentence entirely (it is almost always padding).

    - First-person narrator ('I keep thinking', 'I see this', 'From my experience', 'What I find interesting'). Third-person analytical only.

    - Second-person address. NEVER speak to the reader as 'you' or use commands directed at the reader: 'you should consider', 'you might think', 'imagine you are a CEO', 'picture this scenario', 'as you read this'. The Brightbeam voice is third-person observational. The reader is implicit, never invoked. Even rhetorical or hypothetical 'you' is banned.

    - Podcast/medium framing as context ('on the [X] podcast', 'last Thursday on [show], [person] said...'). Cite sources briefly and factually; never foreground the medium or the occasion.

    - FALSE CONTRASTS — ALL forms. This is the hardest pattern to eliminate; check for each variant explicitly:
      • 'Not X but Y' / 'not from X but Y' / 'not for X but Y'
      • 'The question isn't X, it's Y'
      • 'X isn't just Y — it's Z' (and the variants 'doesn't just capture X. It Y.', 'not only X but also Y')
      • 'Less about X, more about Y' / 'X is more than just Y'
      • 'The constraint is X, not Y' / 'X, not Y' as a punchy closer
      • SOFT STRUCTURAL CONTRASTS (these are the ones that leak): 'Most X. Y is different.' / 'Most enterprises do X — [name]'s work reports something different' / 'Conventional wisdom says X. The data says Y.' / 'X. But Y.' / 'While X, Y' (when used to set up opposition rather than concession)
      • EM-DASH CONTRAST: 'X — but Y' / 'X — yet Y' / 'X — though Y' as the structural pivot
      State Y as a positive construction with its own justification. Do not introduce Y by negating or contrasting against X. If the editorial point genuinely requires acknowledging a prevailing view before stating yours, do it in two separate sentences with no contrast connector ('Many enterprises focus on workflow speed-ups. Sinclair's lab demonstrates a separate use of AI: hypothesis generation.').

    - SOFT IMPERATIVES directed at the reader. Banned: 'Consider...', 'Imagine...', 'Picture...', 'Take a moment to...', 'Ask yourself...', 'Notice that...', 'Think about...', 'Suppose...'. Scott's voice does not instruct the reader to perform mental actions. State the observation directly.

    - Forced tripling — three short clauses or adjectives in a row ('Better stories. More inclusive language. Broader access.' / 'faster, smarter, better' / 'The tools exist. The demand exists. The ROI is measurable.'). Cut to two or expand to one descriptive sentence. Two parallel beats are fine; three is the trip-wire.

    - Reductive fragment chains ('None of this is X. All of it is Y.'). Rewrite in a single measured sentence.

    - Clickbait titles — 'The X Nobody Talks About', 'The Y Nobody Is Making', 'Here's why X', 'The X Problem (And the Fix)'. Titles in Scott's canon are declarative statements or concept labels.

    - Pseudo-profundity ('The key is...', 'The reality is...', 'At its core...', 'The truth is...', 'Here's the thing:').

    - Hollow intensifiers (incredibly, fundamentally, truly, absolutely, deeply, actually, completely, entirely, fully). Remove; the sentence almost always reads better.

    - Aggrieved or strident framing ('deserves more attention', 'the industry keeps missing', 'has been ignored', 'nobody is making'). Analytical, not complaining.

    - Rhetorical question + immediate answer ('Why does this matter? Because...', 'What's the takeaway? Simple:'). Do not write questions you are about to answer yourself.

    - Conclusive overstatement ('this fundamentally changes...', 'this is crucial because...', 'cannot be overstated', 'solves itself').

    - Transition padding ('This is where X comes in', 'Enter: [solution]', 'And that's where things get interesting').

    - Prohibited vocabulary: leverage, utilise, robust, streamline, delve, ecosystem, unlock, harness, paradigm, game-changer, landscape, low-hanging fruit, move the needle, north star, paradigm shift, best-in-class.

    SCAN PROTOCOL — for each emitted field, run these passes in order:
    1. Search for 'matters' (case-insensitive) — if found, restructure or cut.
    2. Search for second-person words ('you', 'your', 'imagine', 'picture', 'consider'). If used as direct address or imperative, rewrite.
    3. Re-read the field for the false-contrast structural shape. If the second sentence/clause exists primarily to negate or set against the first, rewrite as two independent positive statements.
    4. Count consecutive short clauses. If three in a row, cut or expand.
    5. Check the prohibited-vocabulary list and the hollow-intensifier list.
    Apply all five passes BEFORE emitting JSON. The mandatory verification sub-agent will catch leakages but the editor's first job is to not produce them.
